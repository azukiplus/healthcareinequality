Think medical care in America was equitable? Think again.

Hundreds of millions of Americans seek medical care each year

25% of these individuals are minorities

Now imagine you're one of them.

The following are true stories told by real people.

Can you live like they did?

You have been a laborer in Mexico for your entire life, but heard that the USA is a land of economic equality, significantly higher wages, and better opportunities. You are tired of living in an area of crime and poverty, and want to improve your situation and your children's future opportunity. You take your wife and two young children and travel 400 miles via bus to the US border near San Ysidro, CA. You're not sure what is present in this "land of opportunity," but are excited to see what awaits for you and your children's lives ahead.

DAY 1: You are able to cross successfully. Now is the start of your new life in America, the land of opportunity! You are lucky; you have an uncle that lives in Loma Linda who has offered to house your family for the time being.

DAY 2: You and your family can't live in your uncle's house forever, unfortunately. You need to find a job, and fast. Your job options are limited, especially since you don't have a vehicle. These are currently your most favorite job leads:

Construction laborer at a contracting corporation -10 health, $14/hr

Line cook at a Temp catering agency -5 health, $11/hr

Tractor operator at a local strawberry field -8 health, $13/hr

Unfortunately, Latino immigrants have limited job access in the USA. Statistically, construction is the most common profession pursued by Latino immigrants followed by farming & hospitality services. As of 2018, only 26% of Latino immigrants had a Bachelor's degree or more education. Additionally, those with Hispanic accents are less likely to be hired for entry-level positions and are less desirable for managerial promotions.

Unfortunately, poverty and parental absence are strong determinants of child well-being and is very common among immigrant children. You are not alone in having to make these difficult choices.

DAY 6: You're not sure where to go to be seen by a doctor, let alone how to get to one in the first place. WHAT DO YOU DO?

Walk to a local street medicine clinic held by LLU students

Borrow your uncle's car to go to the hospital

You meet with the medical students at street medicine who refer you to the SAC clinic to get checked out for your symptoms. After a physical exam, the physical suggest that you visit the hospital. You ask to borrow your uncle's car to visit.

Healthcare access is substantially more complicated for immigrants than it is for the average American. "Money, because healthcare is not free here... Unless you are coming with refugee or asylee status you can't get Medicaid for 5 years except emergency Medicaid, so cost is a big barrier." -[Vanessa, Washtenaw FQHC] (when asked about the expenses linked to receiving healthcare in the states).

You get pulled over because your uncle's car has a broken tail light. The police officer asks to see your license and vehicle documents. WHAT DO YOU DO?

Tell him that you are going to the hospital, and that it is very urgent

Tell him that you don't have them on you right now

You are ticketed and given an infraction. You are extremely lucky that the officer didn't charge you with a misdemeanor. $-350 CONTINUE

Most undocumented Latino immigrants stress that the presence, as well as the actions, of local police agents from ICE and/or CBP are the main deterrence from traveling to clinics to receive healthcare and social services. "I don't think it's necessarily ICE, I think it is police, particularly the police department. They tend to pull people over for minute things like a broken tail light and then if they are feeling particularly aggressive that day, they could contact ICE." -[Jennifer, Washtenaw FQHC]
